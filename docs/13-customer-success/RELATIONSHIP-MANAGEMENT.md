# Relationship Management

Defines stakeholder mapping, executive relationships, communication cadence, business reviews, customer goals, and relationship risk.

## Stakeholder Mapping

Building on what sales already captured ([SALES-TO-CUSTOMER-HANDOFF.md](../07-sales/SALES-TO-CUSTOMER-HANDOFF.md)): who the actual users are, who champions the relationship internally, and who has budget/renewal authority. A relationship resting on a single contact is fragile — if that person leaves, the account's institutional memory can leave with them unless the map is broader.

## Executive Relationships

For accounts material enough to warrant it, a relationship above the day-to-day user level — someone who can speak to business impact and who customer success or leadership engages periodically, not only when something's wrong.

## Communication Cadence

A stated rhythm for proactive contact, distinct from reactive support interaction — the same principle as [CUSTOMER-COMMUNICATION-STANDARDS.md](../09-operations/CUSTOMER-COMMUNICATION-STANDARDS.md)'s cadence discipline, applied to the ongoing relationship rather than a single ticket.

## Business Reviews

A periodic review with the customer covering progress against their goals ([CUSTOMER-OUTCOMES.md](CUSTOMER-OUTCOMES.md)), usage trends, and what's next — the mechanism that reinforces value continuously rather than only at renewal (per [CUSTOMER-SUCCESS-OPERATING-SYSTEM.md](CUSTOMER-SUCCESS-OPERATING-SYSTEM.md) principle 3). Frequency scales with account significance — not every account needs a formal review, but every account needs *some* proactive touch.

## Customer Goals

The customer's own stated goals (from onboarding, [CUSTOMER-ONBOARDING.md](CUSTOMER-ONBOARDING.md)) are the reference point for every relationship conversation — a check-in that doesn't reference what the customer is actually trying to achieve is generic relationship-building, not customer success.

## Relationship Risk

Signals distinct from usage/health data: a champion who's gone quiet, a new stakeholder unfamiliar with why the product was chosen, organizational change on the customer's side. These often predict trouble before usage data shows it — relationship risk and [health signals](CUSTOMER-HEALTH.md) are read together, not as substitutes for each other.

## Required Artifacts

A stakeholder map per account; a business review record (what was discussed, what's next) for accounts that warrant them; relationship risk notes when a concerning signal appears.

## Ownership

Customer success owns the relationship; sales stays engaged for anything with commercial implications (renewal, expansion); leadership engages for executive-level relationships on significant accounts.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Single point of contact.** Never mapping beyond the one person the account happens to talk to most.
- **Renewal-only contact.** The customer only hearing from the company when a renewal is due.
- **Generic check-ins.** Relationship conversations that don't reference the customer's own stated goals.
