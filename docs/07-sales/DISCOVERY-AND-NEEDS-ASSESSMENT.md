# Discovery and Needs Assessment

Defines how discovery conversations uncover customer pain, current process, business impact, decision process, risks, stakeholders, and success criteria — before any solution is presented. The sales-cycle counterpart to [UX-RESEARCH.md](../05-design/UX-RESEARCH.md): both exist to replace assumption with actual customer evidence.

## Why Discovery Comes First

Presenting a solution before understanding the problem produces a generic pitch that may miss what actually matters to this specific prospect — [DEMO-AND-SOLUTION-PRESENTATION.md](DEMO-AND-SOLUTION-PRESENTATION.md) depends entirely on what discovery uncovers here.

## What Discovery Uncovers

| Area | What to learn |
|------|----------------|
| Current pain | What's actually broken or missing today, in the prospect's own words |
| Current process | How they handle this today, including workarounds |
| Business impact | What this problem costs — time, money, risk, missed opportunity |
| Decision process | Who's involved, how they decide, what approval is needed |
| Risks | What could stall or kill this deal — budget cycles, competing priorities, internal politics |
| Stakeholders | Who else needs to be satisfied beyond the person in the room |
| Success criteria | What "solved" actually looks like to them, specifically |

## Conducting Discovery

- Ask open questions and let the prospect describe their situation before introducing the product — the same "understand before presenting" discipline as [UX-RESEARCH.md](../05-design/UX-RESEARCH.md).
- Confirm business impact in terms the prospect cares about (cost, time, risk) — a problem without a clear cost is hard to justify solving with budget.
- Identify the full stakeholder map early — a deal that looks close but hasn't surfaced its real decision-maker is a deal that will stall later, at the worst time.

## Documenting Discovery

Record findings against each area above, in enough detail that the next conversation (a demo, a proposal) can reference them specifically — "you mentioned X costs your team roughly Y hours a month" is far more effective than a generic recap.

## Using Discovery Findings

- The demo ([DEMO-AND-SOLUTION-PRESENTATION.md](DEMO-AND-SOLUTION-PRESENTATION.md)) is built around what discovery actually found, not a standard deck.
- The proposal's value framing ([PROPOSALS-AND-PRICING.md](PROPOSALS-AND-PRICING.md)) ties back to the business impact discovery quantified.
- A need discovery can't confirm the product actually addresses is a disqualification signal ([IDEAL-CUSTOMER-AND-QUALIFICATION.md](IDEAL-CUSTOMER-AND-QUALIFICATION.md)), surfaced now rather than after a wasted demo.

## Ownership

Salesperson conducts and documents discovery; findings are shared with whoever presents the demo if that's a different person, so nothing gets lost in the handoff.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Feature-dumping instead of discovering.** Talking about the product before understanding whether or how it fits the prospect's actual situation.
- **Skipping stakeholder mapping.** Assuming the person in the room is the full decision-maker.
- **Discovery theater.** Asking the standard questions but not actually using the answers to shape what comes next.
