# Competitive Sales

Defines how sales uses competitive positioning, alternatives, differentiation, battlecards, replacement narratives, and win/loss learning in a live deal. Builds on marketing's [COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md](../06-marketing/COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md) — sales applies that material in real conversations and feeds real outcomes back to it.

## Using Battlecards

[Battlecards](../06-marketing/COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md) are the fast-reference tool for a live conversation — differentiation points and objection responses for a specific competitor, ready before the competitor comes up, not researched in the moment.

## Differentiation in Conversation

Lead with the customer's stated need (from [discovery](DISCOVERY-AND-NEEDS-ASSESSMENT.md)) and show specifically how the product addresses it better for *this* prospect's situation — generic "why we're better" talking points land weaker than a differentiation point tied to what this specific prospect already said matters to them.

## Replacement Narratives

When a prospect is replacing an existing tool or process, address the switching concern directly: migration effort, what's better afterward, and what risk is being managed in the transition. A replacement decision carries more inertia than a greenfield one — acknowledge that rather than ignoring it.

## Handling "We're Also Looking At X"

Ask what's drawing them to the alternative — the answer is often more informative than assuming it's price or a feature gap. Respond with substance and respect; disparaging a named competitor directly reads as insecure (same principle as [BRAND-AND-VOICE.md](../06-marketing/BRAND-AND-VOICE.md)'s "avoid punching down").

## Win/Loss Learning

- Every closed deal — won or lost — records the primary reason, and if competitive, which alternative won or lost against.
- Loss reasons are categorized honestly (price, feature gap, timing, trust, relationship, no decision made) — a loss reason of "price" that's actually a value-communication gap should be recorded as what it really was, not the easy answer.
- Patterns across losses (a specific competitor winning repeatedly on a specific dimension) are escalated to marketing (for positioning) or product (for a real capability gap) — see [COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md](../06-marketing/COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md) and [PRIORITIZATION-STANDARD.md](../02-product/PRIORITIZATION-STANDARD.md).

## Feeding Marketing's Competitive Material

Sales is often the first to hear how a competitor has actually changed (new pricing, new feature, new messaging) — that intelligence feeds back to keep [COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md](../06-marketing/COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md)'s battlecards and comparison content current, closing the loop the same way [MARKETING-TO-SALES-HANDOFF.md](../06-marketing/MARKETING-TO-SALES-HANDOFF.md) does for lead quality.

## Ownership

Salesperson applies competitive material in conversation and documents win/loss reasons; marketing maintains the underlying battlecards and comparison content, informed by sales' real-world intelligence.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Guessing at competitor details.** Stating something about a competitor's product or pricing that hasn't been verified recently.
- **Loss-reason laundering.** Recording every loss as "price" because it's the easiest answer, hiding real patterns.
- **One-way competitive intelligence.** Sales learning things about competitors in the field that never reach marketing's battlecards.
