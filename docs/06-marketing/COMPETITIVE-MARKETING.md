# Competitive Marketing

Defines how competitors, alternatives, customer objections, differentiation, battlecards, comparison content, and market narratives are documented and maintained.

## Competitors and Alternatives

Track both direct competitors (same category, similar solution) and alternatives (different approach to the same problem, including "do nothing" or "build it ourselves") — the second category is often the more common thing a prospect is actually weighing.

## Differentiation

Differentiation is stated as a specific, defensible difference — a capability, an approach, a proof point — not a vague superiority claim. "We integrate with X directly; they require a middleware step" is differentiation; "we're better" is not.

## Customer Objections

Real objections heard from prospects and customers (via sales, once it exists, or support in the meantime — see [CUSTOMER-FEEDBACK-LOOP.md](../09-operations/CUSTOMER-FEEDBACK-LOOP.md)) are documented with the actual response the company wants used to address them — not invented in the abstract without ever having been raised.

## Battlecards

A battlecard is a quick-reference document for a specific competitor: their positioning, their likely strengths, where the company differentiates, and how to respond to common objections about them. Built for whoever needs to respond fast in a real conversation (marketing, and sales once it exists) — dense and current, not a marketing narrative essay.

## Comparison Content

Public comparison pages/content ([CONTENT-MARKETING.md](CONTENT-MARKETING.md)) are factual and fair — every claim about a competitor is checked against their actual current offering, not their offering from a year ago or a strawman version. Inaccurate or unfair comparison content is both an ethical problem and a credibility risk the moment a prospect checks it themselves.

## Market Narrative

The broader story about where the category is going and why the company's approach fits that direction — ties competitive positioning to [POSITIONING-AND-MESSAGING.md](POSITIONING-AND-MESSAGING.md)'s category language rather than existing as a separate, disconnected narrative.

## Maintaining Competitive Material

Competitors change their product and positioning too — battlecards and comparison content are checked for accuracy on a regular cadence, not written once and trusted indefinitely. Stale competitive material that misrepresents a competitor's current state is worse than none.

## Required Artifacts

A battlecard per material competitor; comparison content where a genuine head-to-head evaluation is common; an objections list with company-approved responses.

## Ownership

Marketing owns building and maintaining competitive material; input on real objections and competitor behavior comes from whoever's closest to prospects (support now, sales later); anything published publicly goes through [MARKETING-REVIEW-AND-APPROVAL.md](MARKETING-REVIEW-AND-APPROVAL.md) for fairness and accuracy.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Strawmanning competitors.** Comparing against an outdated or exaggerated version of what a competitor actually offers.
- **Punching down publicly.** Disparaging a competitor by name in a way that reads as insecure rather than confident.
- **Stale battlecards.** Competitive material nobody's checked since the competitor's last major release.
