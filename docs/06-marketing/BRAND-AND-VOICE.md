# Brand and Voice

Defines brand principles, tone, voice, language standards, naming, visual consistency, trust signals, and what the company should avoid saying. Marketing owns this document; [design's content standard](../05-design/CONTENT-AND-MICROCOPY.md) applies the same voice inside the product itself — the two should read as one company, not two.

## Brand Principles

A small set of stated qualities the brand consistently expresses (e.g. direct, expert, approachable) — few enough to actually guide a writing decision, not a long list nobody can hold in mind while writing.

## Tone and Voice

- **Voice** is consistent across all content — how the company always sounds.
- **Tone** adjusts to context (a security incident update is more measured than a feature announcement) while staying recognizably the same voice.
- Match [CUSTOMER-COMMUNICATION-STANDARDS.md](../09-operations/CUSTOMER-COMMUNICATION-STANDARDS.md)'s principles for anything customer-facing: direct, honest about uncertainty, no empty corporate hedge-speak.

## Language Standards

- Plain language over jargon, the same rule as [CONTENT-AND-MICROCOPY.md](../05-design/CONTENT-AND-MICROCOPY.md) — a prospect unfamiliar with the category shouldn't need a glossary to understand the homepage.
- Consistent terminology for the same concept across every surface (website, content, product UI) — two different names for the same thing forces the reader to work out whether they're actually the same.

## Naming

Product, feature, and capability names used publicly match what product has actually named them ([CAPABILITY-MODEL.md](../02-product/CAPABILITY-MODEL.md)) — marketing does not invent a customer-facing name for something without confirming it with product, or the two will diverge the moment a customer talks to support about "the thing on your website" and support doesn't recognize the name.

## Visual Consistency

Marketing surfaces (website, ads, social, decks) use the brand's visual system consistently with the product's [design system](../05-design/DESIGN-SYSTEM.md) — same color/type/logo usage — so a prospect moving from an ad to the website to the product experiences one company, not three.

## Trust Signals

Security/compliance badges, customer logos, testimonials, and certifications are trust signals — each one used publicly is currently accurate and permitted (customer logos with actual consent, security claims that are actually true; see [MARKETING-REVIEW-AND-APPROVAL.md](MARKETING-REVIEW-AND-APPROVAL.md)). An outdated or unauthorized trust signal is worse than none — it's a credibility risk waiting to be noticed.

## What to Avoid

- Overpromising outcomes the product can't reliably deliver.
- Fear-based or manipulative language to drive action.
- Disparaging competitors directly rather than differentiating on substance (see [COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md](COMPETITIVE-MARKETING.md) for how differentiation is handled instead).
- Technical or security claims not verified as currently true.

## Ownership

Marketing owner maintains brand/voice standards; anyone creating public-facing content follows them; design confirms visual consistency with the product's own system.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Voice drift by channel.** Sounding like a different company on social media than on the website.
- **Borrowed tone.** Copying a trendy competitor's voice instead of one that fits this company's actual brand principles.
- **Naming without checking.** Marketing coining a public name for a feature that product calls something else internally, confusing support and customers alike.
