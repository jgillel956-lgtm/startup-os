# Pitch Narrative

Defines how the company story — market problem, product opportunity, traction, business model, moat, team, and use of funds — is translated into an investor narrative. The deck ([INVESTOR-DECK.md](INVESTOR-DECK.md)) is this narrative's visual expression; this document governs the underlying story itself.

## Market Problem

Grounded in the same problem evidence product uses ([CAPABILITY-MODEL.md](../02-product/CAPABILITY-MODEL.md), [PROBLEM.md](../02-product/CAPABILITY-MODEL.md) section) — an investor-facing problem statement that's more dramatic than what product actually validated is a claim that won't survive diligence.

## Product Opportunity

What the product does and why it's the right answer to the stated problem — drawn from actual capabilities and roadmap ([CAPABILITY-MODEL.md](../02-product/CAPABILITY-MODEL.md)), described honestly about what's built today versus what's planned.

## Traction

Real numbers from [SALES-METRICS.md](../07-sales/SALES-METRICS.md) and [CUSTOMER-SUCCESS-METRICS.md](../13-customer-success/CUSTOMER-SUCCESS-METRICS.md) — pipeline, revenue, retention, growth. Traction claims are pulled from these sources, not reconstructed independently for the pitch; if the two ever disagree, the metrics source is correct and the narrative is fixed.

## Business Model

How the company actually makes (or plans to make) money — stated plainly, consistent with [FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md](FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md), not a simplified version that omits an inconvenient dependency or assumption.

## Moat

What makes the business defensible — network effects, data, switching cost, technology, relationships — stated as a specific, examined claim, not "we're just better" or "we move faster." An unexamined moat claim is a red flag investors are trained to probe.

## Team

Why this team is positioned to win — relevant experience and track record, stated factually.

## Use of Funds

Ties directly to the milestones defined in [FUNDRAISING-STRATEGY.md](FUNDRAISING-STRATEGY.md) — an investor can trace exactly what the capital is expected to produce, not a generic "hiring and growth" statement.

## Consistency With Public Narrative

The investor narrative and the public/marketing narrative ([POSITIONING-AND-MESSAGING.md](../06-marketing/POSITIONING-AND-MESSAGING.md)) tell the same underlying story — different emphasis and detail for a different audience, never contradictory facts. An investor who later reads the website or talks to a customer should not discover a different version of the story.

## Required Artifacts

A written narrative outline covering each section above, with its evidence source noted per claim (which metric, which document) — so the deck and any diligence answer can trace back to the same source.

## Ownership

Leadership owns the narrative; marketing supports messaging consistency; sales/customer success supply verified traction; product confirms the opportunity description matches real capability.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Narrative drift from public positioning.** Telling investors a different story than the one told publicly or to customers.
- **Unverified traction numbers.** Pitch metrics that don't trace back to an actual metrics source.
- **Generic moat claims.** Asserting defensibility without a specific, examined mechanism behind it.
