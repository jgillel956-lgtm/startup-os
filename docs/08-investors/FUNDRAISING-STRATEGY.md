# Fundraising Strategy

Defines how a company decides whether to raise, why to raise, how much to raise, what milestones the raise should support, and what readiness checks are required before starting. This document does not provide legal, tax, accounting, or securities advice — decisions about deal structure, terms, and instruments are made with qualified legal and financial advisors.

## Whether to Raise

Raising capital is one option among several (revenue-funded growth, debt, or not growing that dimension yet) — the decision is made deliberately, weighing what the company needs against what dilution, board seats, or reporting obligations cost. "Everyone raises" is not a reason; a specific need the company can't otherwise meet is.

## Why to Raise

A stated reason ties to a specific milestone or capability the capital unlocks — hiring, market expansion, a product bet — not a vague "runway" with no plan for what the runway is for. The "why" is what [PITCH-NARRATIVE.md](PITCH-NARRATIVE.md) and [INVESTOR-DECK.md](INVESTOR-DECK.md) build the use-of-funds story around.

## How Much to Raise

Sized against the milestones the raise is meant to fund plus a reasonable buffer, grounded in [FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md](FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md) — not a round number chosen because it sounds right for the stage. Raising too little risks running out before the milestone lands; raising too much can mean unnecessary dilution or pressure to deploy capital faster than the business is ready for.

## Milestones the Raise Should Support

Named explicitly: what will be true after this capital and time period that isn't true now. These milestones become the backbone of [PITCH-NARRATIVE.md](PITCH-NARRATIVE.md)'s use-of-funds section and, later, [INVESTOR-UPDATES.md](INVESTOR-UPDATES.md)'s progress reporting — the same milestones referenced consistently end to end.

## Readiness Checks

Before actively raising:

- [ ] The narrative and evidence behind it are current and consistent with [PITCH-NARRATIVE.md](PITCH-NARRATIVE.md) and [INVESTOR-DECK.md](INVESTOR-DECK.md).
- [ ] The financial model reflects actual current performance, not stale assumptions ([FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md](FINANCIAL-MODEL-AND-ASSUMPTIONS.md)).
- [ ] The data room is current enough to survive early diligence without scrambling ([INVESTOR-DATA-ROOM.md](INVESTOR-DATA-ROOM.md)).
- [ ] Any claim planned for use (traction, security posture, compliance status) has been verified per [TRUST-COMMUNICATIONS-AND-CLAIMS.md](../14-security-compliance/TRUST-COMMUNICATIONS-AND-CLAIMS.md).

Starting to raise before these checks pass usually costs more time than it saves — an investor conversation stalled by an outdated data room is harder to restart than one that never should have started yet.

## Required Artifacts

A documented fundraising decision: why now, how much, what milestones it funds, and confirmation the readiness checks passed.

## Ownership

Leadership owns this decision; finance (or the founder, at small scale) supplies the financial grounding; qualified legal/tax/securities advisors are consulted on structure and terms before anything is finalized.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Raising reactively.** Starting a raise because an investor reached out, without a stated reason or milestone behind it.
- **Sizing by convention.** Choosing a round size because it's "typical for the stage" rather than because it matches an actual funding need.
- **Raising before readiness.** Taking early investor meetings before the narrative, model, and data room can survive scrutiny.
